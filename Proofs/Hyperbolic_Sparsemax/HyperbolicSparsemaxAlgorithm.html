Full Proof of Hyperbolic Sparsemax

A formal mathematical proof defining the rational, row-stochastic, sparse transition operator built from Lorentzian hyperbolic quadrance and sparsemax projection.

1. Setup

Let the executable lattice be the toroidal $64$-voxel space

$$V=(\mathbb Z/4\mathbb Z)^3.$$

Each voxel $i\in V$ carries a rational spinor

$$q_i=(w_i,\mathbf m_i)\in S^3\cap\mathbb Q^4,$$

where

$$\mathbf m_i=(x_i,y_i,z_i),$$

and

$$w_i^2+\lVert \mathbf m_i\rVert^2=1.$$

Assume the scalar part lies in a rational annulus:

$$0 < w_{\min}\le w_i\le w_{\max}<1.$$

Thus $w_i$ is never $0$ and never reaches the flat limit $1$.

The goal is to define a transition row $S_{i\cdot}$ over a finite candidate neighborhood $\mathcal N_i$, using only rational operations, such that:

  • $S_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$ for every $j\in\mathcal N_i$.
  • $S_{ij}\ge0$.
  • $\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1$.
  • Most $S_{ij}$ are exactly $0$, so the row is sparse.
  • No exponential, square root, trigonometric, or floating-point operation is needed.

2. Rational Lorentz Lift

Definition

Define the Lorentz lift

$$\Phi(q_i)=\tilde q_i = \frac{1}{w_i}(1,\mathbf m_i).$$

Equivalently,

$$\tilde q_i = \left( \frac{1}{w_i}, \frac{x_i}{w_i}, \frac{y_i}{w_i}, \frac{z_i}{w_i} \right).$$

Use the Minkowski bilinear form

$$\langle a,b\rangle_{\mathbb L} = a_0b_0-\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b.$$

Theorem 1: The Lorentz Lift Lands on $\mathbb H^3$

For every rational spinor $q_i=(w_i,\mathbf m_i)\in S^3\cap\mathbb Q^4$ with $w_i>0$, the lift

$$\tilde q_i=\frac{1}{w_i}(1,\mathbf m_i)$$

lies on the positive sheet of the hyperboloid

$$\mathbb H^3 = \{\tilde q:\langle \tilde q,\tilde q\rangle_{\mathbb L}=1,\ \tilde q_0>0\}.$$
Proof

Compute:

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_i\rangle_{\mathbb L} = \left(\frac{1}{w_i}\right)^2 - \left\lVert \frac{\mathbf m_i}{w_i}\right\rVert^2.$$

So

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_i\rangle_{\mathbb L} = \frac{1-\lVert\mathbf m_i\rVert^2}{w_i^2}.$$

Since $q_i\in S^3$,

$$w_i^2+\lVert\mathbf m_i\rVert^2=1.$$

Therefore,

$$1-\lVert\mathbf m_i\rVert^2=w_i^2.$$

Hence,

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_i\rangle_{\mathbb L} = \frac{w_i^2}{w_i^2} = 1.$$

Also,

$$\tilde q_{i,0}=\frac{1}{w_i}>0.$$

So $\tilde q_i\in\mathbb H^3$. Because $q_i$ and $w_i$ are rational, all coordinates of $\tilde q_i$ are rational.

Thus the lift is closed in $\mathbb Q$ and lands on $\mathbb H^3$.

3. Hyperbolic Quadrance

Definition

Define the hyperbolic quadrance between two lifted points by

$$Q_h(i,j) = \langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}^2-1.$$

This avoids the actual hyperbolic distance

$$d_{\mathbb H}(i,j)=\operatorname{arcosh}\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L},$$

which would introduce transcendental functions. Instead, $Q_h$ is a rational polynomial-fraction expression.

Theorem 2: $Q_h$ Is Rational, Symmetric, Nonnegative, and Point-Separating

For all $i,j$,

$$Q_h(i,j)\in\mathbb Q_{\ge0}.$$

Moreover,

$$Q_h(i,j)=Q_h(j,i),$$

and

$$Q_h(i,j)=0 \iff q_i=q_j.$$
Proof

First compute the Lorentz inner product:

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L} = \frac{1}{w_iw_j} - \frac{\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j}{w_iw_j}.$$

Thus

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L} = \frac{1-\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j}{w_iw_j}.$$

Since all coordinates are rational, this is rational. Therefore,

$$Q_h(i,j) = \left( \frac{1-\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j}{w_iw_j} \right)^2 -1$$

is rational.

Symmetry follows because the dot product is symmetric:

$$\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j=\mathbf m_j\cdot\mathbf m_i.$$

So

$$Q_h(i,j)=Q_h(j,i).$$

Now prove nonnegativity. Since $q_i,q_j\in S^3$, their Euclidean dot product satisfies

$$q_i\cdot q_j\le1.$$

But

$$q_i\cdot q_j = w_iw_j+\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j.$$

Therefore,

$$w_iw_j+\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j\le1.$$

Rearranging,

$$1-\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j\ge w_iw_j.$$

Since $w_i,w_j>0$,

$$\frac{1-\mathbf m_i\cdot\mathbf m_j}{w_iw_j}\ge1.$$

Thus

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}\ge1.$$

Hence,

$$Q_h(i,j) = \langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}^2-1 \ge0.$$

Finally, $Q_h(i,j)=0$ iff

$$\langle \tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}=1.$$

On the positive hyperboloid $\mathbb H^3$, this occurs iff

$$\tilde q_i=\tilde q_j.$$

The lift is injective because

$$\tilde q_i=\left(\frac{1}{w_i},\frac{\mathbf m_i}{w_i}\right)$$

recovers

$$w_i=\frac{1}{\tilde q_{i,0}}$$

and then

$$\mathbf m_i=w_i\tilde{\mathbf q}_i.$$

Therefore,

$$\tilde q_i=\tilde q_j \iff q_i=q_j.$$

So

$$Q_h(i,j)=0 \iff q_i=q_j.$$

Therefore $Q_h$ is rational, symmetric, nonnegative, and point-separating.

■ PROVED

4. Rational Hyperbolic Logits

Let $\mathcal N_i$ be the finite candidate neighborhood for source voxel $i$. For each candidate transition $i\to j$, define:

  • inverse temperature: $$\beta_i=\frac{1-w_i}{w_i}\in\mathbb Q,$$
  • torsional resistance: $$A_{ij}=A_0s_{ij}\chi_{ij},$$

where

$$A_0,s_{ij},\chi_{ij}\in\mathbb Q,$$

and usually

$$\chi_{ij}\in\{0,1\}.$$

Define the executable logit

$$z_{i\to j} = -\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij},$$

where

$$\lambda\in\mathbb Q_{>0}.$$

Theorem 3: The Logits Are Rational

For every $j\in\mathcal N_i$,

$$z_{i\to j}\in\mathbb Q.$$
Proof

From Theorem 2,

$$Q_h(i,j)\in\mathbb Q.$$

By assumption,

$$\beta_i,\lambda,A_{ij}\in\mathbb Q.$$

Since $\mathbb Q$ is closed under addition and multiplication,

$$-\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij}\in\mathbb Q.$$

Therefore,

$$z_{i\to j}\in\mathbb Q.$$
■ PROVED

5. Sparsemax Projection

The sparsemax row is defined as the Euclidean projection of the logit vector $z_{i\cdot}$ onto the probability simplex.

Define the simplex

$$\Delta_i = \left\{ p\in\mathbb R^{|\mathcal N_i|}: p_j\ge0,\ \sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}p_j=1 \right\}.$$

Then define

$$S_{i\cdot} = \operatorname{sparsemax}(z_{i\cdot}) = \arg\min_{p\in\Delta_i} \lVert p-z_{i\cdot}\rVert_2^2.$$

Theorem 4: Sparsemax Has Closed Form

The unique minimizer is

$$S_{ij} = [z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+,$$

where

$$[x]_+=\max(x,0),$$

and $\tau_i$ is chosen so that

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+=1.$$

If

$$A_i=\{j:z_{i\to j}>\tau_i\}$$

is the active set, then

$$\tau_i = \frac{\sum_{j\in A_i}z_{i\to j}-1}{|A_i|}.$$
Proof

We solve the constrained optimization problem:

$$\min_{p\in\Delta_i} \frac12\lVert p-z_{i\cdot}\rVert_2^2.$$

The constraints are:

$$p_j\ge0 \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_jp_j=1.$$

Form the Lagrangian

$$\mathcal L(p,\tau,\mu) = \frac12\sum_j(p_j-z_j)^2 + \tau\left(\sum_jp_j-1\right) - \sum_j\mu_jp_j,$$

where $\mu_j\ge0$ are the multipliers for $p_j\ge0$.

Stationarity gives:

$$\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial p_j} = p_j-z_j+\tau-\mu_j = 0.$$

So

$$p_j=z_j-\tau+\mu_j.$$

Complementary slackness gives:

$$\mu_jp_j=0.$$

If $p_j>0$, then $\mu_j=0$, so

$$p_j=z_j-\tau.$$

If $p_j=0$, then feasibility requires

$$z_j-\tau\le0.$$

Therefore,

$$p_j=[z_j-\tau]_+.$$

Finally, $\tau$ is chosen to enforce the simplex condition:

$$\sum_jp_j=1.$$

Thus,

$$\sum_j[z_j-\tau]_+=1.$$

On the active set $A_i=\{j:z_j>\tau\}$, we have

$$\sum_{j\in A_i}(z_j-\tau)=1.$$

Therefore,

$$\sum_{j\in A_i}z_j-|A_i|\tau=1.$$

Solving for $\tau$ gives

$$\tau = \frac{\sum_{j\in A_i}z_j-1}{|A_i|}.$$

Thus the sparsemax solution is $S_{ij}=[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+$.

■ PROVED

6. Rationality of Sparsemax

Theorem 5: Sparsemax Preserves Rationality

If all logits $z_{i\to j}$ are rational, then all sparsemax probabilities $S_{ij}$ are rational.

Proof

By Theorem 3,

$$z_{i\to j}\in\mathbb Q.$$

The active set $A_i$ is determined by sorting and comparing rational numbers. Comparisons of rational numbers are exact.

Once $A_i$ is known,

$$\tau_i = \frac{\sum_{j\in A_i}z_{i\to j}-1}{|A_i|}.$$

The numerator is rational, and $|A_i|$ is a positive integer. Therefore,

$$\tau_i\in\mathbb Q.$$

For every $j$,

$$S_{ij} = [z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+.$$

Since both $z_{i\to j}$ and $\tau_i$ are rational,

$$z_{i\to j}-\tau_i\in\mathbb Q.$$

Taking the positive part either returns that rational value or $0$, so

$$S_{ij}\in\mathbb Q.$$

Therefore sparsemax preserves rationality.

7. Row-Stochasticity and Sparsity

Theorem 6: Sparsemax Rows Are Probability Rows

For every source voxel $i$,

$$S_{ij}\ge0$$

and

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1.$$
Proof

By definition,

$$S_{ij}=[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+.$$

Therefore,

$$S_{ij}\ge0.$$

The threshold $\tau_i$ is chosen precisely to satisfy

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+=1.$$

Thus,

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1.$$

So $S_{i\cdot}$ is a probability row.

■ PROVED

Theorem 7: Sparsemax Produces Exact Sparsity

If

$$z_{i\to j}\le\tau_i,$$

then

$$S_{ij}=0.$$

Therefore sparsemax yields finite exact support.

Proof

If

$$z_{i\to j}\le\tau_i,$$

then

$$z_{i\to j}-\tau_i\le0.$$

Hence,

$$S_{ij}=[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+=0.$$

Thus every inactive candidate receives exactly zero probability, not merely a small value.

■ PROVED

8. Nonempty Support

Theorem 8: The Sparsemax Active Set Is Nonempty

The active set

$$A_i=\{j:S_{ij}>0\}$$

is nonempty.

Proof

Since $S_{i\cdot}\in\Delta_i$,

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1.$$

If every $S_{ij}=0$, then the sum would be $0$, contradicting the simplex constraint.

Therefore at least one $S_{ij}$ is positive, so $A_i$ is nonempty.

9. Hyperbolic Sparsemax Kernel

We now combine the previous pieces.

Definition

The Hyperbolic Sparsemax Kernel is the row operator

$$S_{ij} = \operatorname{sparsemax}_j \left( -\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij} \right).$$

Equivalently,

$$S_{ij} = [z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+,$$

where

$$z_{i\to j} = -\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij},$$

and $\tau_i$ is chosen so that

$$\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1.$$

Main Theorem: Hyperbolic Sparsemax Is Rational, Sparse, and Row-Stochastic

Under the assumptions:

$$q_i\in S^3\cap\mathbb Q^4,$$ $$w_i\in[w_{\min},w_{\max}]\subset(0,1),$$ $$A_0,s_{ij},\lambda\in\mathbb Q,$$

and

$$A_{ij}=A_0s_{ij}\chi_{ij},$$

the Hyperbolic Sparsemax Kernel satisfies:

  • Rationality: $S_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$.
  • Nonnegativity: $S_{ij}\ge0$.
  • Row-stochasticity: $\sum_{j\in\mathcal N_i}S_{ij}=1$.
  • Exact sparsity: $S_{ij}=0 \quad \text{whenever} \quad z_{i\to j}\le\tau_i$.
  • Hyperbolic consistency: The score uses the Lorentz hyperboloid through $Q_h(i,j) = \langle\tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}^2-1$.
  • No transcendental computation: The construction uses only rational arithmetic, comparisons, and positive-part thresholding.
Proof

By Theorem 1, the Lorentz lift is rational and lies on $\mathbb H^3$.

By Theorem 2, $Q_h(i,j)$ is rational, symmetric, nonnegative, and point-separating.

By Theorem 3, the logits $z_{i\to j} = -\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij}$ are rational.

By Theorem 4, sparsemax has the closed form $S_{ij}=[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+$.

By Theorem 5, since the logits are rational, the threshold $\tau_i$ and all entries $S_{ij}$ are rational.

By Theorem 6, $S_{ij}\ge0$ and $\sum_jS_{ij}=1$.

By Theorem 7, all candidates below threshold are assigned exactly zero mass.

No part of the construction uses $e^x,\ \log x,\ \sqrt{x},\ \sin x,\ \cos x,\ \operatorname{arcosh}x$.

Therefore the kernel is rational, sparse, row-stochastic, hyperbolically scored, and executable without transcendental operations.

10. Rational Reverse-Valve Extension

To create a pump, we add a rational asymmetry layer.

Define the rational reverse-valve

$$\rho_{ij} = \frac{1}{1+\lambda A_{ij}},$$

where $A_{ij}\ge0$.

Then $0<\rho_{ij}\le1$. If $A_{ij}>0$, then $\rho_{ij}<1$.

Symmetric Base Walk

Let the sparsemax support determine the admissible undirected edges. On that support choose symmetric rational weights

$$W_{ij}=W_{ji}\in\mathbb Q_{\ge0}.$$

Define

$$d_i=\sum_jW_{ij}, \qquad D=\sum_id_i,$$ $$\pi_i^0=\frac{d_i}{D}, \qquad P_{ij}^0=\frac{W_{ij}}{d_i}.$$

Then $P^0$ is reversible with respect to $\pi^0$.

Proof of Reversibility

Compute:

$$\pi_i^0P_{ij}^0 = \frac{d_i}{D}\frac{W_{ij}}{d_i} = \frac{W_{ij}}{D}.$$

Similarly,

$$\pi_j^0P_{ji}^0 = \frac{d_j}{D}\frac{W_{ji}}{d_j} = \frac{W_{ji}}{D}.$$

Since $W_{ij}=W_{ji}$, we get

$$\pi_i^0P_{ij}^0 = \pi_j^0P_{ji}^0.$$

Thus $P^0$ satisfies detailed balance.

■ PROVED

11. Valve-Modified Pump Kernel

Partition supported oriented edges into forward and reverse sets:

$$E^+ = \{(i,j): W_{ij}>0 \text{ and } i\to j \text{ follows forward chirality}\},$$ $$E^-=\{(j,i):(i,j)\in E^+\}.$$

Define the executable pump kernel $\widehat K$ by

$$ \widehat K_{ij} = \begin{cases} P_{ij}^0, & (i,j)\in E^+,\\ P_{ij}^0\rho_{ij}, & (i,j)\in E^-,\\ P_{ii}^0+\sum_{k:(i,k)\in E^-}P_{ik}^0(1-\rho_{ik}), & j=i. \end{cases} $$

That is: reverse mass is attenuated, and rejected mass is returned to the diagonal.

Theorem 9: $\widehat K$ Is Rational and Row-Stochastic

For every $i,j$,

$$\widehat K_{ij}\in\mathbb Q,$$

and for every $i$,

$$\sum_j\widehat K_{ij}=1.$$
Proof

All quantities $W_{ij},d_i,D,P_{ij}^0,\rho_{ij}$ are rational, so every entry of $\widehat K$ is rational.

Now fix row $i$. Start from the base row:

$$\sum_jP_{ij}^0=1.$$

For each reverse edge $(i,k)\in E^-$, the off-diagonal mass changes from $P_{ik}^0$ to $P_{ik}^0\rho_{ik}$.

The removed amount is

$$P_{ik}^0(1-\rho_{ik}).$$

This removed amount is added to the diagonal. Therefore no mass is lost.

Hence,

$$\sum_j\widehat K_{ij}=1.$$

So $\widehat K$ is rational and row-stochastic.

■ PROVED

12. Broken Detailed Balance and Forward Current

Define the base-referenced current on a forward edge $(i,j)\in E^+$ by

$$J_{ij}^0 = \pi_i^0\widehat K_{ij} - \pi_j^0\widehat K_{ji}.$$

For $(i,j)\in E^+$, $\widehat K_{ij}=P_{ij}^0$, while the reverse transition satisfies $\widehat K_{ji}=P_{ji}^0\rho_{ji}$.

Therefore,

$$J_{ij}^0 = \pi_i^0P_{ij}^0 - \pi_j^0P_{ji}^0\rho_{ji}.$$

Using reversibility of $P^0$,

$$\pi_i^0P_{ij}^0 = \pi_j^0P_{ji}^0 = \frac{W_{ij}}{D}.$$

Thus

$$J_{ij}^0 = \frac{W_{ij}}{D} - \frac{W_{ij}}{D}\rho_{ji}.$$

So

$$J_{ij}^0 = \frac{W_{ij}}{D}(1-\rho_{ji}).$$

Since $W_{ij}\ge0$ and $0<\rho_{ji}\le1$, we get

$$J_{ij}^0\ge0.$$

Moreover, if $W_{ij}>0$ and $\rho_{ji}<1$, then

$$J_{ij}^0>0.$$

Summing over all forward edges,

$$J_0 = \sum_{(i,j)\in E^+}J_{ij}^0 = \sum_{(i,j)\in E^+} \frac{W_{ij}}{D}(1-\rho_{ji}) \ge0.$$

And if at least one forward-supported edge satisfies $W_{ij}>0$ and $\rho_{ji}<1$, then

$$J_0>0.$$

Thus the rational valve breaks detailed balance and produces nonnegative, conditionally strictly positive forward current. Proved.

13. Determinism and Parity Safety

Theorem 10: The Executable Pipeline Is Closed in $\mathbb Q$

Every executable quantity in the Hyperbolic Sparsemax Pump lies in $\mathbb Q$.

Proof

The pipeline consists of:

  1. rational spinors: $q_i\in\mathbb Q^4$
  2. rational Lorentz lifts: $\tilde q_i=\frac{1}{w_i}(1,\mathbf m_i)\in\mathbb Q^4$
  3. rational quadrance: $Q_h(i,j)\in\mathbb Q$
  4. rational logits: $z_{i\to j}\in\mathbb Q$
  5. rational sparsemax thresholds: $\tau_i\in\mathbb Q$
  6. rational sparsemax entries: $S_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$
  7. rational base weights: $W_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$
  8. rational valve factors: $\rho_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$
  9. rational pump entries: $\widehat K_{ij}\in\mathbb Q$

Every step uses only rational field operations, comparisons, and max with $0$. Therefore the entire executable pipeline remains in $\mathbb Q$.

Corollary: Parity Safety

If all rational numbers are represented canonically, then two nodes with the same seed compute identical rows $S_{i\cdot}$ and identical pump rows $\widehat K_{i\cdot}$.

Therefore no floating-point drift can alter the discrete parity invariant, such as

$$\operatorname{torusWinding}(\text{mantle})\bmod 7=\text{core}.$$

14. Derrick-Stable Annulus

The scalar $w_i$ controls the inverse temperature

$$\beta_i=\frac{1-w_i}{w_i}.$$

Since $w_i\in[w_{\min},w_{\max}]\subset(0,1)$, we get finite bounds:

$$\frac{1-w_{\max}}{w_{\max}} \le \beta_i \le \frac{1-w_{\min}}{w_{\min}}.$$

Therefore,

$$0<\beta_{\min}\le\beta_i\le\beta_{\max}<\infty.$$

So the system avoids both limits:

  • $w_i\to0^+$ gives $\beta_i\to\infty$, causing hard collapse.
  • $w_i\to1^-$ gives $\beta_i\to0$, causing uniform diffusion.

The annulus prevents both. Ties are resolved either by bounded rational perturbation of $\beta_i$ or by deterministic canonical ordering, such as lexicographic voxel-channel order.

Thus the executable kernel remains stable and deterministic.

Theorem

Under the assumptions:

$$V=(\mathbb Z/4\mathbb Z)^3,$$ $$q_i=(w_i,\mathbf m_i)\in S^3\cap\mathbb Q^4,$$ $$w_i\in[w_{\min},w_{\max}]\subset(0,1),$$ $$A_{ij}=A_0s_{ij}\chi_{ij}\in\mathbb Q_{\ge0},$$

and

$$\lambda\in\mathbb Q_{>0},$$

the Hyperbolic Sparsemax construction

$$\tilde q_i=\frac{1}{w_i}(1,\mathbf m_i),$$ $$Q_h(i,j)=\langle\tilde q_i,\tilde q_j\rangle_{\mathbb L}^2-1,$$ $$z_{i\to j}=-\beta_iQ_h(i,j)-\lambda A_{ij},$$ $$S_{ij}=[z_{i\to j}-\tau_i]_+$$

defines a rational, sparse, row-stochastic executable kernel.

When equipped with a symmetric rational base walk $P^0$ and rational reverse-valve

$$\rho_{ij}=\frac{1}{1+\lambda A_{ij}},$$

the valve-modified kernel $\widehat K$ remains rational and row-stochastic and satisfies

$$J_0 = \sum_{(i,j)\in E^+} (\pi_i^0\widehat K_{ij}-\pi_j^0\widehat K_{ji}) \ge0.$$

Moreover,

$$J_0>0$$

whenever at least one supported forward edge has nonzero affinity and nontrivial reverse attenuation.

Therefore the Hyperbolic Sparsemax Pump is a deterministic, rational, parity-safe executable counterpart to the analytic hyperbolic softmax pump, with sparse geometric collapse, broken detailed balance, and bounded annular stability.
