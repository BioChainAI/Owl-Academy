The Clifford Torus

Built from Five Rings of Prerequisite Knowledge

Scope

This document proves and explains the mathematical foundations of the Clifford torus, beginning from linear algebra in $\mathbb{R}^4$ and building toward its role as a flat torus inside the unit $3$-sphere $S^3$.

We will focus only on the Clifford torus for now, but we will prepare the ground for later sections involving quaternions and irrational winding.

1. Euclidean 4-space

Definition

The real vector space $\mathbb{R}^4$ is the set of ordered quadruples

$$ \mathbb{R}^4=\{(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2):x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2\in\mathbb{R}\}. $$

We write a point or vector as $p=(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)$.

The standard dot product on $\mathbb{R}^4$ is

$$ p\cdot q=x_1u_1+y_1v_1+x_2u_2+y_2v_2, $$

where $p=(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2), \qquad q=(u_1,v_1,u_2,v_2)$.

The Euclidean norm is

$$ \|p\|=\sqrt{p\cdot p} = \sqrt{x_1^2+y_1^2+x_2^2+y_2^2}. $$

The Euclidean distance between two points $p,q\in\mathbb{R}^4$ is $d(p,q)=\|p-q\|$. Thus $\mathbb{R}^4$ is a metric space with metric:

$$ d(p,q)=\sqrt{(x_1-u_1)^2+(y_1-v_1)^2+(x_2-u_2)^2+(y_2-v_2)^2}. $$

2. Orthogonal decomposition of $\mathbb{R}^4$

We decompose $\mathbb{R}^4$ into two orthogonal coordinate planes:

$$ P_1=\{(x_1,y_1,0,0):x_1,y_1\in\mathbb{R}\}, $$

and

$$ P_2=\{(0,0,x_2,y_2):x_2,y_2\in\mathbb{R}\}. $$

Every point $p\in\mathbb{R}^4$ decomposes uniquely as $p=p_1+p_2$, where $p_1=(x_1,y_1,0,0)\in P_1$ and $p_2=(0,0,x_2,y_2)\in P_2$.

These planes are orthogonal because if $p_1\in P_1$ and $p_2\in P_2$, then $p_1\cdot p_2=0$.

Therefore, $\|p\|^2=\|p_1\|^2+\|p_2\|^2$. Explicitly, $\|p\|^2=x_1^2+y_1^2+x_2^2+y_2^2$.

This decomposition is the linear-algebraic backbone of the Clifford torus: the torus is built by taking one circle in $P_1$ and another circle in $P_2$.

3. The unit 3-sphere $S^3$

Definition

The unit $3$-sphere in $\mathbb{R}^4$ is

$$ S^3=\{(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)\in\mathbb{R}^4:x_1^2+y_1^2+x_2^2+y_2^2=1\}. $$

Equivalently, $S^3=\{p\in\mathbb{R}^4:\|p\|=1\}$. Although $S^3$ sits inside $4$-dimensional space, it is itself $3$-dimensional. It is the $4$-dimensional analogue of the ordinary sphere $S^2=\{(x,y,z)\in\mathbb{R}^3:x^2+y^2+z^2=1\}$.

4. $S^3$ as a smooth level set

Define the smooth function $F:\mathbb{R}^4\to\mathbb{R}$ by $F(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)=x_1^2+y_1^2+x_2^2+y_2^2$. Then $S^3=F^{-1}(1)$.

The gradient of $F$ is $\nabla F=(2x_1,2y_1,2x_2,2y_2)$. At any point $p\in S^3$, we have $\|p\|=1$, so $p\neq 0$. Therefore $\nabla F(p)\neq 0$.

By the regular level set theorem, $S^3=F^{-1}(1)$ is a smooth $3$-dimensional submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^4$.

Conclusion

The Clifford torus will be a $2$-dimensional surface sitting inside this $3$-dimensional sphere $S^3$.

Ring 2: Differential Geometry of Surfaces

5. Parametrized surfaces

A parametrized surface in Euclidean space is a smooth map

$$ X:U\subset\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^n. $$

Here the parameter domain $U$ is usually described by coordinates such as $(\theta,\gamma)$.

For the Clifford torus, the natural parameters are two angles:

$$ \theta\in\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}, \qquad \gamma\in\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}. $$

That means $\theta$ and $\gamma$ are periodic with period $2\pi$.

6. The first fundamental form

Given a parametrized surface $X(\theta,\gamma)$, the tangent vectors are

$$ X_\theta=\frac{\partial X}{\partial \theta}, \qquad X_\gamma=\frac{\partial X}{\partial \gamma}. $$

The first fundamental form is the metric induced on the surface from the ambient Euclidean space. Its coefficients are:

$$ E=X_\theta\cdot X_\theta, $$ $$ F=X_\theta\cdot X_\gamma, $$ $$ G=X_\gamma\cdot X_\gamma. $$

Thus the induced metric is

$$ ds^2=E\,d\theta^2+2F\,d\theta\,d\gamma+G\,d\gamma^2. $$

This tells us how distances are measured within the surface.

7. Intrinsic versus extrinsic geometry

A surface has two kinds of geometry:

  • Extrinsic geometry: how the surface bends inside a larger ambient space.
  • Intrinsic geometry: what can be measured by someone living entirely on the surface.

For example, a cylinder in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is extrinsically curved, but intrinsically flat. If you cut it open and unroll it, it becomes a flat rectangle without stretching.

Gaussian curvature $K$ is intrinsic. If $K=0$, the surface is intrinsically flat.

The key Clifford torus claim is:
The Clifford torus is intrinsically flat.
This does not mean it is flat as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^4$. It means its induced metric has zero Gaussian curvature.

Ring 3: Complex Numbers and the Product of Circles

8. The circle $S^1$

The unit circle in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is

$$ S^1=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x^2+y^2=1\}. $$

Using complex numbers, we identify $\mathbb{C}\cong\mathbb{R}^2$ by $z=x+iy$.

Then the unit circle is $S^1=\{z\in\mathbb{C}:|z|=1\}$.

The standard parametrization is $z=e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$.

So $S^1=\{e^{i\theta}:\theta\in\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}\}$.

9. The product $S^1\times S^1$

The product of two circles is $S^1\times S^1$.

A point of $S^1\times S^1$ is an ordered pair $(e^{i\theta},e^{i\gamma})$. This is a $2$-dimensional torus.

To embed it into $\mathbb{C}^2$, define $(\theta,\gamma)\mapsto (e^{i\theta},e^{i\gamma})$. Since $\mathbb{C}^2\cong\mathbb{R}^4$, we may write

$$ (e^{i\theta},e^{i\gamma}) = (\cos\theta,\sin\theta,\cos\gamma,\sin\gamma). $$

This lies in $\mathbb{R}^4$. However, its norm is

$$ \cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma=2. $$

So this version lies on the sphere of radius $\sqrt{2}$ in $\mathbb{R}^4$, not the unit sphere $S^3$.

To place it on $S^3$, we scale both circles by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.

Ring 4: The Clifford Torus Itself

10. Definition of the standard Clifford torus

The standard Clifford torus is the subset of $\mathbb{R}^4$ given by

$$ T_{\mathrm{Cl}} = \left\{ (x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)\in\mathbb{R}^4: x_1^2+y_1^2=\frac{1}{2}, \quad x_2^2+y_2^2=\frac{1}{2} \right\}. $$

Equivalently,

$$ T_{\mathrm{Cl}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}S^1\times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}S^1. $$

A parametrization is

$$ X(\theta,\gamma) = \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cos\theta, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sin\theta, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cos\gamma, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sin\gamma \right). $$

11. Proof that the Clifford torus lies in $S^3$

We compute the squared norm:

$$ \|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2 = \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cos\theta\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sin\theta\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cos\gamma\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sin\gamma\right)^2. $$

Thus

$$ \|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2 = \frac{1}{2}(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + \frac{1}{2}(\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma). $$

Since $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ and $\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma=1$, we get

$$ \|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2 = \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}=1. $$

Therefore $X(\theta,\gamma)\in S^3$.

Conclusion

The Clifford torus is a $2$-dimensional torus embedded in the unit $3$-sphere $S^3\subset\mathbb{R}^4$.

12. General rectangular Clifford tori

More generally, choose positive radii $r_1,r_2>0$ satisfying $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$.

Define

$$ T_{r_1,r_2} = \left\{ (x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)\in\mathbb{R}^4: x_1^2+y_1^2=r_1^2, \quad x_2^2+y_2^2=r_2^2 \right\}. $$

A parametrization is

$$ X(\theta,\gamma) = (r_1\cos\theta,r_1\sin\theta,r_2\cos\gamma,r_2\sin\gamma). $$

Then

$$ \|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2 = r_1^2(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + r_2^2(\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma) = r_1^2+r_2^2=1. $$

So $T_{r_1,r_2}\subset S^3$. The standard Clifford torus corresponds to $r_1=r_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.

Ring 5: Intrinsic Flatness of the Clifford Torus

13. Induced metric of $T_{r_1,r_2}$

Let $X(\theta,\gamma) = (r_1\cos\theta,r_1\sin\theta,r_2\cos\gamma,r_2\sin\gamma)$.

Compute the tangent vectors:

$$ X_\theta = (-r_1\sin\theta,r_1\cos\theta,0,0), \qquad X_\gamma = (0,0,-r_2\sin\gamma,r_2\cos\gamma). $$

Now compute the metric coefficients. First,

$$ E=X_\theta\cdot X_\theta = r_1^2\sin^2\theta+r_1^2\cos^2\theta = r_1^2. $$

Second, $F=X_\theta\cdot X_\gamma$. Because $X_\theta$ lives entirely in the first coordinate plane and $X_\gamma$ lives entirely in the second coordinate plane,

$$ F=0. $$

Third,

$$ G=X_\gamma\cdot X_\gamma = r_2^2\sin^2\gamma+r_2^2\cos^2\gamma = r_2^2. $$

Therefore the induced metric is

$$ ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2. $$

14. Proof that the Clifford torus is intrinsically flat

The metric $ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2$ has constant coefficients.

Define new coordinates $u=r_1\theta, \qquad v=r_2\gamma$.

Then $du=r_1\,d\theta, \qquad dv=r_2\,d\gamma$. So,

$$ ds^2=du^2+dv^2. $$

This is exactly the Euclidean metric on a flat rectangle.

The only difference is that the coordinates are periodic: $\theta\sim\theta+2\pi, \quad \gamma\sim\gamma+2\pi$.

Therefore, $u\sim u+2\pi r_1, \quad v\sim v+2\pi r_2$.

So the torus is intrinsically equivalent to a flat rectangle with opposite sides identified:

$$ [0,2\pi r_1]\times[0,2\pi r_2]. $$

Since the Euclidean plane has Gaussian curvature $K=0$, and the metric is locally Euclidean, the Clifford torus has $K=0$.

Conclusion: Every torus $T_{r_1,r_2}$ of the form $X(\theta,\gamma) = (r_1\cos\theta,r_1\sin\theta,r_2\cos\gamma,r_2\sin\gamma)$, where $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$, is intrinsically flat. In particular, the standard Clifford torus is intrinsically flat.

Ring 6: Golden Ratio Scaling

Although the standard Clifford torus uses equal radii, the documents introduce a nonstandard torus whose radii satisfy a golden ratio relation.

Let

$$ \frac{r_1}{r_2}=\varphi, \quad \text{where} \quad \varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}. $$

We also require the torus to lie on $S^3$, so $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$.

Since $r_1=\varphi r_2$, we substitute:

$$ (\varphi r_2)^2+r_2^2=1 \implies (\varphi^2+1)r_2^2=1. $$

Using the golden ratio identity $\varphi^2=\varphi+1$, we get $\varphi^2+1=\varphi+2$.

Therefore $r_2^2=\frac{1}{\varphi+2}$, so

$$ r_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}. $$

Then

$$ r_1=\varphi r_2 = \frac{\varphi}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}. $$

Conclusion

The golden-ratio-scaled torus has radii

$$ r_1=\frac{\varphi}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}, \qquad r_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}. $$

Its parametrization is

$$ X(\theta,\gamma) = \left( \frac{\varphi}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}\cos\theta, \frac{\varphi}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}\sin\theta, \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}\cos\gamma, \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varphi+2}}\sin\gamma \right). $$

It still lies on $S^3$ because $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$. It is still intrinsically flat because its induced metric is $ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2$, which becomes Euclidean after the coordinate change $u=r_1\theta, \quad v=r_2\gamma$.

Ring 7: Irrational Winding on the Torus

15. Linear flow on the torus

A winding path on the torus is given by

$$ \theta(t)=\theta_0+\alpha t, \qquad \gamma(t)=\gamma_0+\beta t. $$

Substituting into the torus parametrization gives

$$ X(t) = (r_1\cos(\theta_0+\alpha t), r_1\sin(\theta_0+\alpha t), r_2\cos(\gamma_0+\beta t), r_2\sin(\gamma_0+\beta t)). $$

This describes a linear flow on the torus.

16. Closed versus dense winding

Because both angles are periodic modulo $2\pi$, the path closes if there exists some $T>0$ such that $\alpha T\in 2\pi\mathbb{Z}$ and $\beta T\in 2\pi\mathbb{Z}$.

That means there exist integers $m,n$ such that $\alpha T=2\pi m$ and $\beta T=2\pi n$. Dividing gives:

$$ \frac{\alpha}{\beta}=\frac{m}{n}. $$

So if $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\in\mathbb{Q}$, the path is periodic and closes.

If instead $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\notin\mathbb{Q}$, then no such common period exists. The path never closes.

More strongly, a standard theorem on irrational linear flows on the torus says: If $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\notin\mathbb{Q}$, then the trajectory is dense in $S^1\times S^1$.

That means for every point $p$ on the torus and every neighborhood $U$ of $p$, the winding path eventually enters $U$.

17. Why the golden ratio matters

The golden ratio $\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$ is irrational. Therefore, if the winding rates satisfy

$$ \frac{\alpha}{\beta}=\varphi, $$

then the trajectory is nonperiodic and dense in the torus. So the golden ratio is not needed to define the Clifford torus itself. It matters when one wants a non-repeating winding rule.

Important distinction

There are two different uses of ratios:

  1. Radius ratio $\frac{r_1}{r_2}=\varphi$. This changes the rectangular shape of the flat torus.
  2. Winding-rate ratio $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=\varphi$. This makes the winding path nonperiodic and dense.

These are related in some speculative protocols, but mathematically they are distinct choices.

Final Theorem: The Clifford Torus

Let $r_1,r_2>0$ satisfy $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$.

Define $X:S^1\times S^1\to S^3\subset\mathbb{R}^4$ by $X(\theta,\gamma) = (r_1\cos\theta,r_1\sin\theta,r_2\cos\gamma,r_2\sin\gamma)$.

Then:
1. $X(S^1\times S^1)$ lies in $S^3$.
2. $X(S^1\times S^1)$ is a smoothly embedded torus.
3. Its induced metric is $ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2$.
4. It is intrinsically flat.
5. If a path winds with angular rates $\alpha,\beta$ and $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational, then the path is dense in the torus.

Proof

First, compute the squared norm: $\|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2 = r_1^2(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + r_2^2(\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma)$. Using $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ and $\cos^2\gamma+\sin^2\gamma=1$, we obtain $\|X(\theta,\gamma)\|^2=r_1^2+r_2^2=1$. Hence $X(\theta,\gamma)\in S^3$.

Second, the image is the product of two circles: $x_1^2+y_1^2=r_1^2, \quad x_2^2+y_2^2=r_2^2$. Therefore it is diffeomorphic to $S^1\times S^1$. So it is a smooth embedded torus.

Third, compute tangent vectors: $X_\theta = (-r_1\sin\theta,r_1\cos\theta,0,0)$, and $X_\gamma = (0,0,-r_2\sin\gamma,r_2\cos\gamma).$ Thus $X_\theta\cdot X_\theta=r_1^2$, $X_\theta\cdot X_\gamma=0$, and $X_\gamma\cdot X_\gamma=r_2^2$. Therefore the induced metric is $ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2$.

Set $u=r_1\theta, \quad v=r_2\gamma$. Then $ds^2=du^2+dv^2$. So the metric is locally Euclidean, and hence the Gaussian curvature is $K=0$. Thus the torus is intrinsically flat.

Finally, a linear winding path has the form $\theta(t)=\theta_0+\alpha t, \quad \gamma(t)=\gamma_0+\beta t$. If $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\in\mathbb{Q}$, then the path closes. If $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\notin\mathbb{Q}$, then the path is dense in the torus by the classical irrational-flow theorem on $S^1\times S^1$. Therefore all claims follow.

Clean Summary

The Clifford torus is not just an ordinary donut drawn in 3-space. It is the product of two circles embedded in $\mathbb{R}^4$:

$$ T_{r_1,r_2} = \{(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2):x_1^2+y_1^2=r_1^2,\ x_2^2+y_2^2=r_2^2\}. $$

When $r_1^2+r_2^2=1$, it lies on the unit $3$-sphere $S^3$. Its induced metric is $ds^2=r_1^2\,d\theta^2+r_2^2\,d\gamma^2$. After the coordinate change $u=r_1\theta, \quad v=r_2\gamma$, this becomes $ds^2=du^2+dv^2$.

Therefore the Clifford torus is intrinsically flat.
