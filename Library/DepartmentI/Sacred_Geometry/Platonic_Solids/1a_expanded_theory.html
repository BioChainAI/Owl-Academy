01

The Space Everything Lives In

Before you can describe a shape, you need somewhere to put it.

Geometry is the study of shapes and space. Everything we'll build sits inside a kind of "stage." To understand that stage, we need one idea: dimension. Dimension just means how many directions you can move.

What is "Euclidean Space"?

Simply Put: Normal, Flat Reality

You will often hear math teachers talk about "Euclidean Space" (named after an ancient Greek guy named Euclid). Don't let the fancy name scare you. It just means normal, everyday, flat space.

It is the kind of space where a flat table sits on a flat floor, and if you draw two straight lines next to each other, they will never crash into each other. It's the 3D world you walk around in every single day.

How we say "where": Coordinates

To pin down a location in this normal 3D space, we use coordinates. Think of coordinates as a GPS address for a single tiny speck. The starting point, where everything reads zero, is called the origin. We use three numbers, usually written (x, y, z).

Don't be scared of the symbol ℝ³

ℝ³ (say "R-three") is just shorthand for three-dimensional space. The ℝ stands for the real numbers (every measurement you could find on a ruler), and the ³ means "three of them" (width, height, depth). When you see "ℝ³ volumes," just read it as "solid objects in a 3D room."

02

The Three Building Blocks

Almost any shape breaks down into just three kinds of pieces.

The word primitive means "a simplest building block, something you can't break down any further." In 3D geometry, there are three of them, and they stack on top of each other.

Vertex (Corner)

A single point in space. Pure position, infinitely small. It is defined entirely by its coordinates (like a GPS address).

Everyday Example: A single tiny dot made by the tip of a very sharp pencil.

Edge (Line)

A straight line connecting two vertices. It has length, but no thickness.

Everyday Example: A taut piece of string pulled tightly between two thumbtacks.

Face (Surface)

A flat surface bordered by edges, with vertices at its corners. It has area, but no thickness.

Everyday Example: A perfectly flat sheet of glass, or a piece of paper.

Why designers are obsessed with triangles

Notice how the Face example above is a triangle? Any three dots in space will always form a perfectly flat surface. You can't bend a triangle out of flatness. But four or more dots might not line up flat (think of a wobbly chair with 4 legs). The triangle is the only shape guaranteed to stay perfectly flat. That's why 3D models in games are built almost entirely out of tiny triangles.

03

Wrapping a Surface into a Solid

A face is flat and thin. So how do we get to a solid you could actually hold?

The trick is to enclose space. If you arrange flat faces so they completely surround a region with absolutely no gaps, then the empty space trapped inside becomes a volume (a solid object).

This approach has a name: boundary representation (often shortened to B-rep). The idea is simple but powerful: you describe a solid only by describing its skin. The skin tells the computer exactly where the "inside" is.

Simply Put: The Hollow Chocolate Bunny

Think of a hollow chocolate bunny. You don't need solid chocolate all the way through to know it is shaped like a bunny. The thin shell of chocolate on the outside is enough to define the shape. In 3D computer graphics, everything is a hollow chocolate bunny. We just build the shell out of flat faces.

A Hidden Pattern

Take any solid made of flat faces, count its pieces, and do this basic math:
(Corners) minus (Edges) plus (Faces).
You will always, always get the number 2. This is Euler's formula.

V − E + F = 2

vertices − edges + faces = two

06

Curves: Exact vs Approximate

A cube is easy. How do you describe a perfectly round ball?

A true sphere has zero flat faces and zero straight edges. You can't list its corners because it doesn't have any. So, computers that need to draw shapes quickly often fake the curve instead.

They cover the ball in hundreds of tiny flat triangles, a process called tessellation. The resulting patchwork is called a mesh.

Simply Put: Like Pixels on a TV

Think about a video game on a TV screen. If you get really close to the TV, you can see that Mario is actually just made of tiny square blocks (pixels). But from the couch, he looks smooth. Tessellation is the exact same thing, but for 3D shapes. Computers build a "fake" ball out of flat blocks.

07

Why does this matter?

These three simple building blocks run the modern digital world.

Video Games

Characters are just thousands of flat triangles (faces) drawn 60 times a second.

Manufacturing

Car engines are designed using exact geometry so parts fit together perfectly.

3D Printing

Printers read the hollow outer faces (B-rep) to know where to squirt plastic.

Robotics

Robots use these shapes to know if they are going to bump into a wall.
