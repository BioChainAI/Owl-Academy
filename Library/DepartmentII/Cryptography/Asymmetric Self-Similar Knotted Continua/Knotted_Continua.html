Generation of infinite multiscale chiral structures via a three-state graph-directed system.

Abstract

We describe a formal framework for constructing asymmetric self-similar continua in $\mathbb{R}^3$ using trefoil knots as local geometric units. The construction is governed by an asymmetric directed Markov transition graph and tiered contraction ratios, producing an embedded object modeled as the attractor of a graph-directed iterated function system.

We establish a finite set of states $\mathcal{S}=\{S_1,S_2,S_3\}$. Admissible recursive transitions are strictly dictated by the directed adjacency matrix $M$:

$$ M= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Because $M \ne M^T$, the transition structure is decidedly non-reversible. This creates a recursive flow where State 1 branches into States 2 and 3, State 2 flows only into State 3, and State 3 loops back exclusively to State 1.

Exhibit A: Directed Asymmetric Markov Graph. S1 commands dual forward flow, establishing primary hierarchy, while the S3->S1 return loop forces infinite recursive knotting.

Instead of scaling flat lines or spheres, our primitive seed $K_0 \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ is the standard $(2,3)$-torus knot. It is mathematically described by the parametric equations:

$$ \begin{aligned} x(t)&=(2+\cos 3t)\cos 2t \\ y(t)&=(2+\cos 3t)\sin 2t \\ z(t)&=\sin 3t \end{aligned} $$

This introduces fundamental chirality at the lowest level of the manifold. Its mirror image cannot be isotopically aligned with itself without entering a 4th dimension.

Exhibit B: The $(2,3)$-Torus Seed. A continuous line crossing itself 3 times, possessing an inherent handedness that breaks spatial symmetry.

In classical Iterated Function Systems (IFS) like the Sierpinski Triangle, a single shape is composed of smaller copies of itself ($X = \cup T_i(X)$). The Graph-Directed Attractor shatters this singular self-similarity.

Instead, we have a family of distinct topological spaces ($X_1, X_2, X_3$). Their internal compositions are dictated strictly by the transitions mapped in the matrix $M$.

"$X_1$ does not contain smaller copies of $X_1$; rather, it acts as a boundary containing the geometry of $X_2$ and $X_3$. The recursion only loops back to itself indirectly through the deep-layer transition of $S_3 \to S_1$. This generates an asymmetric, multi-tiered fractal where each layer inherits properties from a different parent state."

The entire recursive object is defined simultaneously via the Hutchinson operator for the three topological states:

$$ X_i=K_0^{(i)}\cup \bigcup_{\{j:M_{ij}=1\}} T_{ij}(X_j), \qquad i=1,2,3. $$

Applying the specific $M$ matrix, the mutually dependent recursion unfolds as:

$$ \begin{aligned} X_1 &= K_0^{(1)}\cup T_{12}(X_2)\cup T_{13}(X_3) \\ X_2 &= K_0^{(2)}\cup T_{23}(X_3) \\ X_3 &= K_0^{(3)}\cup T_{31}(X_1) \end{aligned} $$

Assuming equal contraction ratios $c$ across all transitions, the weighted adjacency matrix is $A(s) = c^s M$. The Hausdorff dimension $d$ is determined by finding where the spectral radius equals $1$:

$$ \rho(A(d)) = 1 \implies c^d \rho(M) = 1 \implies d = \frac{\log \rho(M)}{-\log c} $$

The characteristic polynomial of $M$ is $\lambda^3 - \lambda - 1$. Its dominant real root is the Plastic Constant:

$$ \rho(M) \approx 1.324717957 $$

Unlike symmetric IFS fractals modeled by the Golden Ratio, the asymmetric topology here is governed strictly by the Plastic Constant, generating a unique space-filling geometry.
